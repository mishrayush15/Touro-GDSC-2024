General behavior practices

Local Customs and Traditions:

- Respect - Respect is highly valued in Japanese culture. Politeness and consideration for others are essential.

- Shoes Off Indoors -- It's customary to remove shoes when entering someone's home, traditional ryokans (inns), and certain traditional accommodations.

- Gift Giving - Gift giving is a common practice to express gratitude. Gifts are often presented and received with both hands, and it's polite to show humility when receiving a gift.

- Onsen Etiquette - When using hot springs (onsen), thoroughly wash and rinse your body before entering the communal bath. Tattoos may be a concern in some onsen, so it's good to check the rules in advance.

- Queuing - Japan is known for orderly queues. Always wait in line and avoid cutting in front of others.

















Tipping Practices:

- No Tipping Culture - Tipping is not a common practice in Japan. Exceptional service is expected, and tipping can be considered rude. Instead, high-quality service is included in the overall experience.

- Exceptional Service - If you receive exceptional service and want to show appreciation, a polite thank you is more appropriate than tipping. Exceptional service is considered a part of the job.
Greetings and Gestures:

- Bowing - Bowing is a common form of greeting and showing respect. The depth of the bow varies depending on the context and relationship.

- Handshakes - Handshakes are also used, especially in more formal or business settings. However, it's a good idea to follow the lead of the Japanese person regarding physical greetings.

- Eye Contact - Maintain moderate eye contact during conversations. Excessive eye contact may be perceived as confrontational.

- Gift of Business Cards - When exchanging business cards (meishi), offer and receive them with both hands. Take a moment to study the card before putting it away. This practice is important in business settings.

- Silence - Silence is often valued in conversations. Pauses are considered thoughtful, and interrupting can be seen as impolite.
